## How Did AI Tools Affect Your Production Pipeline?

**Before AI tools, this task would have required:**
- A professional animation team to produce even a few seconds of 2D anime-style footage
- Weeks of storyboarding, character design iteration, and frame-by-frame drawing
- Separate tools for scriptwriting, visual design, and video editing with no integration
- Large budget for voice acting, sound design, and post-production

**With AI tools (即梦AI + Lovart / image generation), the process became:**
- Writing detailed text descriptions translated directly into usable video frames and clips
- Iterating on visual direction quickly by refining prompts rather than redrawing assets
- One person able to produce multiple video segments across prologue and two endings
- The creative bottleneck shifted from "can we make this look good?" to "can we describe it precisely enough?"

**Unexpected challenges:**
- Maintaining consistent character appearance across clips — 即梦AI + Lovart does not automatically remember what Mr. Star or the other characters look like between generations, so each prompt must re-describe the character fully
- The gap between the "cute anime style" vision and AI output sometimes required multiple iterations, especially for complex emotional expressions (e.g., Mr. Star's bittersweet face on the cake mountain)
- Motion prompts are harder to predict than image prompts — camera movement in particular often required several attempts to achieve the intended feel

## What Were the Implications of Including AI Tools in Your Output?

**Creative implications:**
Working with AI required translating visual instincts into precise language. This changed how I thought about scenes — rather than sketching, I had to write. This actually deepened my understanding of what I wanted each shot to communicate, because I could not be vague. On the other hand, I sometimes accepted AI-generated results that were "close enough" rather than exactly right, because the cost of re-generating was friction. This is a real creative compromise.

**Quality implications:**
The AI produced results that would have been impossible to achieve manually at this scale. The dungeon atmosphere, the transformation sequences, and the emotional lighting were consistently impressive. However, fine details — specific character expressions, exact outfit consistency, subtle emotional nuance — were harder to control. The overall quality was high for an academic project, but a professional production would need more curation and iteration passes.

**Authorship implications:**
The storyboard prompts, the narrative choices, the emotional arc, and the scene-by-scene decisions are all mine. The AI executed my descriptions. In that sense the creative work is mine — but the execution is collaborative with a machine that has its own aesthetic tendencies I cannot fully predict or control. I think this is a genuinely new kind of authorship: directive rather than generative, curatorial rather than executional.

## Key Learnings

1. **Precision in language is a creative skill.** Writing a good AI video prompt is not just description — it is direction. Knowing what to emphasize (lighting, camera angle, emotional beat, character position) and what to leave open is a skill that takes practice.

2. **Consistency requires a system.** For a multi-clip project, establishing a style base phrase (e.g., *"cute anime style, 2D hand-drawn animation, soft cell shading, warm dungeon lighting"*) and reusing it as a prefix on every prompt saved significant iteration time and improved visual consistency.

3. **Story structure shapes everything downstream.** The decision to have two divergent endings (A = redemption / B = food world) made every earlier scene in the prologue carry double meaning — Mr. Star's gluttonous behavior is both comedic setup and tragic foreshadowing. Getting the story logic right first made writing the prompts much easier.

## If I Did It Again…

I would establish character reference sheets *before* writing any prompts — a single canonical image description for each character that could be copy-pasted into every prompt. The inconsistency risk across 17 clips (9 prologue + 4 ending_a + 4 ending_b) is the biggest quality risk in this project, and a standardized character block would have addressed it from the start.

I would also plan the ending B "food world" transformation scene earlier in the process, because it required the most creative imagination (what does a world made of food look like, visually, in cute anime style?) and benefited most from extra iteration time.
